The story in one line

From the classroom to the ballot box — and beyond

Voting is a right, not a reflex. It has to be learned, felt, practised and kept. This is how we introduce democracy at every life stage — small, age-right deliveries from KS2 to 35 — so the habit built in school actually carries through to adulthood, where today it falls away.

Why a continuous journey

Engagement is built in school — then lost in early adulthood

Jersey runs strong school programmes and lets Islanders vote from 16. But participation drops sharply once people leave the classroom. The most disengaged group is everyone under 35 — exactly where the pipeline currently has no next step.

The journey · five stages

One spine, five age-right deliveries

Each stage introduces the next idea about democracy, with activities pitched to that age — and each one connects back to the same live Assembly business adults will meet later. Nothing is taught twice; everything builds.

1
Ages 7–11Primary
Key Stage 2 · The seed

“We decide together, and it’s fair.”

Concept introduced

Democracy is how a group makes fair choices when people want different things — everyone gets a say, the most-supported choice wins.

Made concrete by

Class votes, school council elections, deciding a shared rule — first-hand experience of a vote that actually changes something around them.

Piecemeal delivery
🗳 Class & school-council votes📚 “Having a say” story unit⚖ Fairness games
✦
Builds: the gut feeling that my voice counts — the emotional foundation everything else stands on.
2
Ages 11–14Lower secondary
Key Stage 3 · The map

“This is who decides — and where.”

Concept introduced

Jersey is governed by people we choose: the States Assembly, Ministers and the parishes. Decisions are made by a process you can name and follow.

Made concrete by

A visit to the Assembly or parish hall, a mock debate on a real question, meeting a Deputy — seeing the chamber as a real place, not an abstraction.

Piecemeal delivery
🏛 Assembly / parish visit🎤 Mock debate🗺 “How Jersey is run” explainer
✦
Builds: a mental map of who holds power and how it’s used — so later, “the Assembly” means something.
3
Ages 14–16GCSE years
Key Stage 4 · The stakes

“These decisions land on me.”

Concept introduced

Assembly business isn’t remote — it’s housing, wages, health fees, transport and the environment. The things you’ll feel as an adult are decided now.

Made concrete by

Working a real proposition (e.g. social-housing rents, the living wage) through a mock scrutiny panel; a school-wide mock election with genuine issues.

Piecemeal delivery
🏠 Real-issue case studies🔍 Mock scrutiny panel✅ School mock election
✦
Builds: the link between a vote and my own life — answering the “what’s this got to do with me?” question early.
4
Ages 16–18Sixth form
Key Stage 5 · The right, exercised

“I can vote — now, for real.”

Concept introduced

In Jersey you can vote from 16. The right stops being a lesson and becomes a real choice you make on a real ballot — and a habit you start.

Made concrete by

Auto/assisted registration at school, a guided first vote, Q&A with sitting Members, and how to use Scrutiny, petitions and contacting a Member.

Piecemeal delivery
📝 Register-to-vote drive🗳 Guided first vote👥 Meet your Members
⭐ The pivot: lesson becomes action — capture the very first vote while we still reach them
✦
Builds: a first vote on the record — and people who vote once are far likelier to vote again.
5
Ages 18–35Early adulthood
Adulthood to 35 · Keeping the habit

“It still has something to do with me.”

Concept introduced

No school now reminds you. So the Assembly comes to you — live business translated into plain “what this means for you” terms, in the places adults already are.

Made concrete by

The “What’s It To Me?” plain-language layer: short, impartial explainers in English and key community languages, each ending in a real action — pushed to workplaces, parish hubs and social feeds.

Piecemeal delivery
📱 Plain-language explainers🌐 Community-language versions➡ One-tap routes to act
✦
Builds: the bridge across the gap — sustaining the school-built habit through the years turnout currently collapses.
The break we’re fixing

Today the pipeline stops at the school gate

Jersey already does KS2–KS5 well. The failure point isn’t the teaching — it’s that there is no stage 5. Strong school programmes don’t translate into adult participation because the moment a young person leaves education, every prompt to engage disappears.

Where it breaks

At 18, the school council, the mock election and the citizenship lesson all stop at once. The right remains — the reminders, the plain language and the “why it matters” do not. Reach falls away fastest exactly here.

Result: 17% under‑35 turnout, worst in town among renters and EAL Islanders.

How stage 5 closes it

“What’s It To Me?” is the missing rung — it carries the same idea taught in school (these decisions land on you) into adult life, on the channels adults actually use, always pointing to an action they can already take.

One continuous habit: say → map → stakes → first vote → kept vote.
Why it works as one journey

A habit, not a one-off campaign

Each stage is light on its own — but joined up, they turn a right people have into a right people use. Three principles hold the whole spine together.

A

Same thread throughout

The same idea — “these decisions land on you” — is reintroduced age-appropriately at every stage, so adulthood feels like the next step, not a cold start.

B

Always a real action

From a class vote to a real ballot to a Scrutiny submission, every stage ends in doing, not just knowing. Participation is rehearsed until it’s normal.

C

Meets people where they are

School reaches the young; the plain-language layer reaches the adults school no longer can — closing the reach gap in town and in community languages.

The goal isn’t a better turnout number in one election. It’s a generation that grows up expecting to have a say — and never loses the prompt to use it.
