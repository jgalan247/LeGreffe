The problem

Most Islanders never connect with the Assembly that shapes their lives

Engagement is strong for young people and at election time. The space in between — and the adults in it — is where reach falls away.

~32%
turnout, June 2026 (official, Vote.je)
27,691 voted — the most in over a decade — yet with auto-registration, fewer than 1 in 3 on the roll did

The young aren’t carrying through

Under-35 turnout was just 17% in 2022 — strong school programmes aren’t translating into adult participation.

And it’s worst in town

St Helier ~22% vs rural St Mary ~53% — renters, younger and EAL Islanders, who vote least, cluster in town.

“What’s this got to do with me?”

Assembly business feels remote from housing, wages, schools and roads — the things people actually feel.

“Who can actually read this?”

Propositions and debates are long and dense — the facts exist, but not in a form most people will read.

“Where would I even see it?”

Engagement waits on the website for people to come to it — not the places adults already are.

The proposed solution

A plain-language layer that brings live Assembly business to people

Don’t create more content — translate what already exists into plain, personal, accessible terms, take it to where people are, and point them to the action they can already take.

1

Translate

Turn live propositions into short “what this means for you” explainers — AI-assisted, always human-checked for impartiality.

2

Place

Push them where adults already are — workplaces, parish halls, community hubs and social feeds — in plain English and key community languages.

3

Activate

End every explainer with a clear route into existing tools: Scrutiny, petitions, contacting a Member.

✓
Impartiality is built in. The AI only drafts; a human officer reviews and signs off every word before it publishes. Explainers describe and attribute — they never advocate — and each links back to the official record.
Try it

The pipeline, working on a real proposition

Draft an explainer in the Officer console, tick the impartiality checks, approve it — and watch it appear for readers. Example: social housing rents and “rental stress” (P.29/2025).
