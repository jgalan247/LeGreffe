A proposal for discussion

Bringing the Assembly's work to the Islanders it reaches least

This is a starting point rather than a finished plan — an initiative I'd like to think through with you, and one I'd expect to change as we test it.

It builds directly on something you've already seen: MyTurn, the civics trainer for young Islanders. The question this proposal opens is what comes after school — how the same habit of engagement carries through to the adults the Assembly currently reaches least.

1 · The gap

Where engagement tends to fall away

It's worth being careful with the 2026 headline. Turnout was widely reported as having "fallen" from 41.6% to 32.4% — but that drop is largely arithmetic, not a collapse in interest. In raw numbers, more Islanders voted than in 2022, and the most in over a decade.1

What changed is the measure. Automatic registration added roughly 25,000 names to the roll, so the same act of voting is now counted against a much larger electorate. The more useful reading is the opposite of the headline: auto-registration has, for the first time, put almost every eligible Islander on the register — which makes visible just how many are not yet taking part. That, rather than a falling percentage, is the engagement gap.

And the gap is patterned, not random. In 2022 turnout among tenants was 16% against 41% for owner-occupiers, and in 2026 it ran from 21.9% in St Helier to 52.6% in rural St Mary.2 Renters, younger and English-as-an-additional-language Islanders — who vote least — tend to cluster in town. These are the people this initiative is for.

Building on MyTurn

You've seen MyTurn working at the school-age end of this journey, and it's encouraging that it resonated. That success is also what frames the gap: if engagement is healthy in school but under-35 turnout sits at 17%, the open question is what happens once young people leave that environment — and how we keep the connection alive into adult life.

Talking to people, I think we'd hear three things again and again — and they're worth testing rather than assuming:

"What's this got to do with me?"

Assembly business can feel remote from housing, wages, schools and roads — the things people actually feel day to day.

"Who can actually read this?"

Propositions and debates are long and dense. The facts exist, but not always in a form most people will sit down and read.

"Where would I even see it?"

Engagement often waits on the website for people to come to it, rather than meeting them where they already are.

2 · The idea

A plain-language layer, rather than more content

My instinct is not to create yet more material, but to translate what already exists into plain, personal terms, take it to where people are, and point them towards the action they can already take. The Assembly produces a great deal of good information; the difficulty is the form it arrives in. So the proposal is really about a thin layer on top of existing work, not a new stream of it.

1

Translate

Turn live propositions into short "what this means for you" explainers — drafted with AI assistance, and always checked by a person for accuracy and impartiality.

2

Place

Take them to where adults already are — workplaces, parish halls, community hubs and social feeds — in plain English and key community languages.

3

Activate

End every explainer with a clear route into tools that already exist: sharing views with Scrutiny, signing or starting a petition, contacting a Member.

The part I'd most want to get right

Impartiality. For the Greffe this isn't a feature, it's the whole basis of trust — so I'd build the process around a human sign-off: AI only ever drafts, an officer reviews and approves every word before anything is published, explainers describe and attribute rather than advocate, and each one links back to the official record. I'd genuinely welcome the panel's view on where that line should sit.

3 · An example

What one explainer might look like

To make it concrete, here is a sketch of a single explainer on a real proposition — social housing rents and "rental stress" (P.29/2025). It is illustrative, and every word would be officer-checked before publishing.

What's it to me?
The Assembly, in plain language · EN / PT
Housing

Social housing rents: what's being decided?

What's proposed
From 2027, social housing rents would be set so they don't leave tenants in "rental stress" — paying over 30% of their income on rent.
The case for
Supporters say it would protect lower-income tenants and could reduce money spent on income support.
The case to weigh
Others note any change must stay financially sustainable for housing providers and Government, so costs need working out first.
Where it stands
The Assembly agreed to review the policy. No change to rents has been made yet.
What you can do
Share your views with Scrutiny↗
Sign or start a petition↗
Find and contact your Members↗

A sketch, not a live tool — shown to convey the format and tone. A fuller working prototype exists if you'd like to explore it, but the proposal stands on the idea, not the build.

Why this shape

It leads with the everyday question, not the proposition number. It gives both sides plainly and attributes them. It's honest about status — "no change has been made yet" — which matters for trust. And it closes on something a reader can actually do today.

Why bilingual from the start

A meaningful share of the Islanders who vote least speak Portuguese or Polish at home. Offering explainers in key community languages isn't an add-on — it's much of the point. I'd want to confirm which languages to prioritise with the team and community partners.

4 · Planning

How I'd approach planning it

I'd rather start small and learn than launch broadly and hope. So my approach would be a contained pilot, designed from the outset to be evaluated, with the explicit option of stopping or reshaping it if the evidence doesn't support scaling.

Start with a pilot

One topic that people clearly feel — housing is a natural candidate — in one parish, over roughly six to eight weeks. Small enough to run carefully, real enough to learn something true.

Work through partners

The Greffe doesn't need to reach everyone directly. Parishes and Connétables, Scrutiny, employers, libraries and community-language groups already have the trust and the footfall — the role is to equip them.

Assumptions I'd want to test first

  • Whether a plain-language explainer actually changes how understood or able to act people feel — not just whether they read it.
  • Which channels genuinely reach the adults we're missing, rather than the already-engaged.
  • How much officer time the review-and-sign-off step realistically takes, at the pace propositions move.
  • Where the impartiality line sits in practice on a genuinely contested topic.
5 · Delivery

Putting it into practice

If the pilot is encouraging, this is roughly how it could move from a single topic to something sustained — with a decision point built in, not assumed.

Phase 1weeks 1–8

Pilot

One topic, one parish. Draft, review and publish a small set of explainers; place them through partners; set a clear baseline to measure against.

Phase 2weeks 9–12

Listen and evaluate

Gather reach and feedback, talk to partners and readers, and be honest about what didn't work. Decide openly whether to continue, adjust or stop.

Phase 3if supported

Decide and scale

Extend to more topics, parishes and languages at a pace the sign-off process can sustain — keeping quality and impartiality ahead of volume.

Cost

Modest. The publishing layer is light and builds on tools and channels that largely exist; the real investment is officer time on review.

Main risk

An inaccurate or one-sided explainer would do real damage to trust. The review gate exists precisely to carry that risk — which is why it's never optional.

6 · Discussion

Knowing whether it works — and where I'd welcome your steer

I'd want to be honest that engagement is hard to attribute, so I'd measure modestly and read the numbers with care rather than claim too much from them.

Reach

How many people see an explainer, where, and in which language — broken down enough to tell whether we're reaching new people or the already-engaged.

Action

How often a reader follows through to a real step — a Scrutiny submission, a petition, an email to a Member — as a sign the "activate" part is landing.

The long view

A baseline against which to watch participation over time, held loosely — turnout has many causes, and this would only ever be one of them.

The bigger picture this sits in

The ambition isn't a single tool but a continuous habit of engagement, from the classroom through to adult life. MyTurn already does real work at the young end; this proposal is about the adult end of the same journey.

Questions I'd genuinely like to talk through with you

  • Where would you draw the line between explaining a proposition and being seen to take a side?
  • Which topic, and which part of the Island, would be the most useful place to start?
  • Are there partners — parishes, employers, community groups — you'd reach for first?
  • What would make this feel like a success to you, twelve months in?

None of this is fixed. I've brought a clear starting point on purpose, but the value is in shaping it together — so I'd rather end on these questions than on conclusions.
